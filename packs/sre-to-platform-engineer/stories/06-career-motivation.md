# STAR Stories — Career and Motivation

**Category 6 of 6 · 3 prompts**

> **Terms used in this category**
> - **STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Result
> - **SRE** — Site Reliability Engineer
> - **Platform Engineer** — the role you're transitioning to
> - **Career narrative** — your coherent story of why you've done what you've done, and what this next role fits
> - **Point of view** — a specific, defensible opinion that signals independent thinking
>
> Full definitions and equivalent titles in the [pack README](../README.md#what-we-mean-by-sre).

These last three prompts are where the interviewer learns whether you're a candidate with a coherent career narrative or one who happens to be interviewing. "Why platform, why now, and what would you change" are the questions most likely to be asked, and most likely to be answered generically.

The content here isn't career advice — it's specific guidance for *how* to answer well, assuming you've already made the decision to pursue platform roles.

---

## Prompt 18 — Why platform engineering?

> *"Why platform engineering, for you?"*

### SRE trap

Common weak patterns:

- Generic: "I like infrastructure work and platform engineering is the next step" — could be said by anyone, reveals no specific thinking
- Framework-heavy: "According to the CNCF report, platform engineering is a growing field" — industry analysis is not a personal answer
- Career-mechanical: "It's the next title, and I want to grow" — technically honest but reveals no internal motivation

### Platform framing

Interviewers want a specific answer that could only be given by you. Four ingredients of a strong answer:

1. **The moment(s) in your career that taught you platform work mattered to you.** Not a bullet list; a concrete specific memory — a team you helped, a problem you solved, a colleague's reaction.
2. **The shape of the work you enjoy most.** Designing, implementing, enabling, teaching, debugging, reasoning about organizations — most platform engineers have one or two of these as their center of gravity. Name yours.
3. **The loop you want to optimize for.** Many platform engineers describe some version of: "I build something; another engineer I don't know uses it months later in a way I wouldn't have predicted; they tell me it made their work better." Your version of that loop.
4. **The honest piece** — what you're giving up by moving into this role, and why that trade-off is right for you now.

### Coaching rubric (1–4 per criterion)

- **Specific moment** — Not generic; a particular memory or event
- **Shape of work** — Named your center of gravity
- **Loop you want** — Articulated the feedback loop that motivates you
- **Honest trade-off** — Named what you're giving up

Target: 3+ on each.

### Example shape

> "I've been an SRE for 9 years. For most of that time, the thing I liked most was not the on-call work — it was when other engineers reached for something I'd built and told me it made their day-to-day easier. The specific moment I think about: I'd built a small internal library for consistent structured-logging, and about a year later an engineer I'd never met joined a different team and asked me a clarifying question about it, in a way that made it obvious it had become part of how her team operated. That loop — build something, release it, watch it become part of other people's defaults — is what I want more of.
>
> The shape of work I'm best at is the design-and-enable mix. I'm an OK implementer, a good designer, and a very good interlocutor with other teams about what they actually need. Staff-level infrastructure IC work doesn't give me enough of the latter two; platform engineering puts them at the center of the role.
>
> What I'm giving up: the satisfying quality of SRE work in incident response — the adrenaline, the specific skill of triage, the end-of-shift clarity when the thing is resolved. I'll miss that. Platform work is longer-loop and less conclusive. I've decided that's the right trade-off for this decade of my career, and I'll still carry on-call at platform teams. But it's not the same."

## Prompt 19 — Why now?

> *"Why this move, now?"*

### SRE trap

Common weak patterns:

- "The market is strong for platform roles" — external-drive answer; reveals no internal push
- "My current company doesn't have platform opportunities" — reads as running *from* rather than *to*
- "I've been thinking about it for a while" — vague; reveals no specific catalyst

### Platform framing

Interviewers are not testing whether your timing is optimal. They're testing whether you've thought about it enough to have a non-generic answer. Strong answers include:

1. **A specific catalyst or moment** — a reorg, a project that changed your sense of what you want, a mentor's comment, a role you watched unfold, a piece of work that reframed you
2. **The preparation you've actually done** — reading, thinking, talking to platform engineers, building relevant things on the side, the 12 weeks of deliberate prep you've been doing
3. **Why now and not 2 years ago** — what's changed, internally or externally
4. **Why now and not 2 years from now** — what would be lost by waiting

### Coaching rubric (1–4 per criterion)

- **Specific catalyst** — Not vague; a particular trigger
- **Evidence of preparation** — Something concrete you've done
- **Why not earlier** — Acknowledged the past decision not to move
- **Why not later** — Named the opportunity cost of waiting

Target: 3+ on each.

### Example shape

> "Two things converged in the last year. First, our SRE team took on a formal platform-product charter — we were asked to treat our deploy tooling as a product for internal teams, not an ops tool for ourselves. I found I was the one most drawn to that part of the work, volunteering for the conversations with product teams, writing the internal docs, and noticing when the tooling was accidentally SRE-centric. That's the work I want to do more of.
>
> Second: several colleagues in my network have been navigating role transitions after reorgs in the last year. It caused me to think about what I want to be doing, not just what I'm doing well — and I realized my center of gravity had shifted. I've been preparing deliberately for about 3 months — reading, doing a 12-week self-directed curriculum on platform engineering topics I wasn't already deep in, and informally talking with platform engineers at 4 different companies about how their role actually works day-to-day.
>
> Why not 2 years ago: I was still learning things in the SRE-IC track that were valuable. I'm glad I didn't rush. Why not 2 years from now: the overlap between my SRE experience and the platform role is sharp right now. In 2 years I'd be 2 years further from operational work, and the translation would be harder, not easier."

## Prompt 20 — What would you change about your current role / team / org?

> *"If you could change one thing about how your current team or org operates, what would it be and why?"*

### SRE trap

Two common weak patterns:

- Complain: "We have too much tech debt and no one prioritizes it." Feels like venting; reveals no agency.
- Deflect: "Everything's fine, really. I've had a great experience." Reads as unwilling to criticize — which interviewers read as incapable of the critical thinking they need.

### Platform framing

This prompt is a test of:

1. Whether you have honest observations about the organizations you've been part of
2. Whether you can articulate them without bitterness or blame
3. Whether you have a perspective on what *better* looks like, not just what's wrong
4. Whether your answer is specific enough to be real

Strong answers are often about a structural thing, not a personality. They acknowledge why the current way exists (usually, reasonable history) and offer a specific alternative, with an honest trade-off.

### Coaching rubric (1–4 per criterion)

- **Specific, not generic** — A concrete thing, not "communication"
- **Acknowledges why it is the way it is** — Doesn't straw-man the current state
- **Better alternative named** — Offered a specific change, not just a complaint
- **Trade-off honest** — The alternative has costs; named them

Target: 3+ on each.

### Example shape

> "One thing I'd change: our platform-ish work — the deploy tooling, the shared CI, the observability baseline — is scattered across the SRE team, one product group's infra team, and one specific senior engineer's 20% time. There's no single team that owns the *internal developer experience* as a product, and that shows up in subtle ways: the tools don't quite connect, the ownership fades at the seams between them, nobody is strictly responsible for making the whole workflow coherent.
>
> It exists this way for good reasons. The org grew up in a phase where platform-as-product wasn't how people talked about this work, and SRE was the natural home for 'tools other engineers use.' That was the right call for the time.
>
> What I'd change is to consolidate these scattered pieces under one small platform team with a clear product charter. The trade-off: that team becomes a bottleneck for a while — the SRE team and the product-group infra team would need to hand off their pieces, and some of the seams would be worse before they were better. And consolidating costs head-count decisions that are always contentious.
>
> I say this knowing I'd probably be someone who could contribute to that team, so there's self-interest in the answer. But it's an observation I made before I was thinking about my own next move — I've mentioned it in planning conversations at work, so it's not a pitch I'm giving for the first time in this interview."

## How to use this file

1. Read all three prompts. These are unlikely to map 1:1 to past events — they require you to articulate a perspective on your career arc.
2. For each, draft an answer that is *yours* and *specific*. If yours sounds generic, rewrite it until it couldn't apply to anyone else.
3. Record yourself delivering each in 1.5–2 minutes (these should be a bit shorter than STAR stories). Listen back for sincerity, not polish.
4. Score with the rubric.
5. Revisit in Week 12 one day before interviews — these specifically benefit from being re-read fresh, not over-rehearsed.

### The "point of view" question

The final prompt — *"tell me something you believe about platform engineering that many of your peers disagree with"* — is covered in [Week 12](../weeks/week-12-behavioral.md) rather than here, because it is meta to the 20 prompts. The 20 STAR prompts produce evidence; the point-of-view question produces a perspective. Both matter; they are different work.
