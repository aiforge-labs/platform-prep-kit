# Week 12: Behavioral + Closing

**Phase 4 — Leadership, Interviews, Closing**

> **Terms used in this week**
> - **SRE** — Site Reliability Engineer
> - **Platform Engineer** — the role you're transitioning to
> - **STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Result; the canonical behavioral-interview story format
> - **Behavioral interview** — an interview format that probes past experience as a predictor of future performance (typically 45–60 minutes, 4–6 prompts)
> - **Closing** — the 5–10 minute "do you have any questions for me?" segment at the end of most interviews
> - **Reference check** — the post-offer conversation between the hiring manager and your former colleagues/managers
>
> Full definitions and equivalent titles in the [pack README](../README.md#what-we-mean-by-sre).

Week 12 is the final week and the narrowest — not because it's unimportant, but because most of the content is practice and polish on material you've already generated. The 20 STAR prompts in [`../stories/`](../stories/) are where this week lives.

The goal this week isn't to memorize answers. It's to:

1. Pick your strongest 8 stories out of the 20
2. Practice them until they're natural, not rehearsed
3. Learn to pivot — if an interviewer asks a question that doesn't match a pre-prepared story, you can adapt
4. Come into interview day physically and mentally ready, not over-rehearsed

---

## Time commitment

~6 hours — deliberately less than other weeks. Over-preparing the day before is a common mistake.

- Review and pick your 8 stories: 1.5 hours
- Practice (out loud): 2–3 hours
- Closing-question research: 1 hour
- Rest, taper, don't over-prep: the rest

## Learning objectives

By end of week you should be able to:

1. Deliver any of your 8 strongest STAR stories in 2–3 minutes, naturally
2. Pivot — identify which story fits an unexpected prompt within 10 seconds
3. Answer "why platform engineering?" and "why now?" in your own voice, not a template
4. Ask 3–5 closing questions that reveal senior signal (without being performative)
5. Know what to do with a bad interview — the recovery story you'll tell yourself, not the rumination

Deliberately light. Don't add new material this week.

### Required

**1. Review your STAR prompts**

- 20 minutes.
- Tag each prompt with a story from your own career. Some will be easy; some will require digging. If you can't find a story for a prompt, either (a) you haven't reflected enough — take 30 minutes to think, or (b) it's genuinely not your experience — note which one and move on.

**2. In-repo reference — behavioral quiz bank**

### Why this little

You've already done the work in Weeks 1–11. Week 12 is refinement, not new material. If you feel you need to read more, that's usually a signal you're avoiding the harder work of *practicing out loud*.

## Apply (3 hours) — Pick Your 8, Practice Them, Pivot

### Part A — Pick your 8 (30 min)

From the 20 prompts in [`../stories/`](../stories/), pick the 8 you will lead with. Aim for balance across the six categories:

- 1–2 Technical leadership
- 1–2 Customer / developer empathy
- 1–2 Reliability-to-platform translation
- 1 Influence without authority
- 1 Failure story (critical — never skip this category)
- 1 Career and motivation

Write each on an index card or a one-line note. For each, include:

- The prompt it directly answers
- The 1–2 prompts it can also cover (with 10-second reframing)
- The specific number or measurable outcome that anchors it
- The surprising or non-obvious detail that makes it memorable

### Part B — Practice out loud (2 hours)

Don't read; speak.

1. **Round 1 (60 min):** walk through all 8 stories, timed 2–3 minutes each. Record if possible. Listen back. The gap between how you sound and how you think you sound is always larger than expected.
2. **Round 2 (30 min):** pivot drills. Pick 5 prompts at random (use a list or a peer). Your job: identify which of your 8 stories covers the prompt, reframe the opening sentence to directly answer it, and deliver in 2 minutes. Tests your flexibility.
3. **Round 3 (30 min):** the hardest stories. Redo whichever stories you were weakest on in Round 1. Often: the failure story. Give yourself a second try on the failure — the first telling is usually too defensive; the second is usually tighter and more honest.

### Part C — Closing questions (45 min)

Interviewers almost always end with "Do you have questions for me?" Strong candidates treat this as *part of* the interview, not a courtesy.

Prepare 5–7 questions, with 3–5 of them tailored to the company / role / interviewer. Good questions for platform-engineering roles:

- *"What's the platform team's 'hardest open problem' right now? The one that's been on the roadmap for a quarter and no one has quite cracked."*
- *"How does the platform team measure success? What's the leading indicator you're watching?"*
- *"How do you handle the tension between platform opinions and team autonomy when they conflict?"*
- *"What's an example of something the platform team recently chose *not* to build? Walk me through the decision."*
- *"Who does the platform team report to organizationally, and who funds its headcount? (Good answer: a platform-focused executive or the CTO. Yellow flag: matrixed across 4 product teams, which often means no clear backing.)"*
- *"What's the team's on-call situation? How often does the platform wake someone up?"*
- *"If I take this role, what would you expect me to get done in my first 90 days?"*

Avoid:

- Questions answerable on the company website
- "What's the culture like?" (too vague; reveals no thought)
- Compensation or logistics questions in the technical round — save those for the recruiter or the final round
- Questions that sound rehearsed; better to have 10 candidates in your head and ask whichever fits the conversation

### Part D — Why platform, why now (30 min)

Write and rehearse your answer to these two questions. They come up in most platform-engineering interviews, usually in the first or last 10 minutes.

**"Why platform engineering?"**

- Weak: a generic answer about enjoying infrastructure work.
- Strong: a *specific* answer grounded in what you've learned about yourself. "I've always liked building things other engineers use, but the actual satisfaction comes when a team I don't know sends me a Slack message months later saying the thing I built made their on-call less painful. That's the loop I want to optimize for."
- Requires honesty: why does this role fit, when you have other options?

**"Why now?"**

- Weak: generic answer about market conditions or wanting a new challenge.
- Strong: an honest answer about where you are in your career and what this role gives you that you can't get elsewhere. Reflect real context — you've been an SRE for X years, you see platform work as the natural extension, you've been building toward this (see the 12 weeks of prep you just did).

### Part E — The recovery story (15 min)

Every interview loop has at least one bad round. Seniors know this. Prepare, mentally:

- What you will tell yourself after a bad round (not ruminate — just acknowledge and move on)
- What you will tell yourself after a bad *whole loop* (often: you learned something about the role / the company / yourself; the next one is closer)
- A plan for the next 48 hours after any interview: debrief once, write down learnings, then stop. No rumination loops.

Interview loops are as much about energy management as about performance. Plan the recovery; don't improvise it.

## Articulate (30 min) — The Meta-Questions

Interviewers often end or start with one of these. Have an answer ready — not a script, an answer you've thought about.

1. *"What's the hardest part of platform engineering, for you?"*
2. *"What's a piece of feedback you've gotten that changed how you work?"*
3. *"Tell me something you believe about platform engineering that many of your peers disagree with."*

The third is the "point of view" question — the one that distinguishes a candidate with senior opinions from one who just knows the material. Candidates who can't answer it often read as "has read the books but hasn't formed a perspective." Strong answers don't have to be contrarian; they have to be *specific* and *yours*.

Examples of real perspectives (use your own — don't adopt any of these):

- *"Most platform teams I've seen over-invest in Backstage before they have three things worth cataloging. Portals are the fifth thing you build, not the first."*
- *"Cost observability is a top-3 platform capability in 2025, and most platforms still treat it as 'someone else's problem.'"*
- *"The best platform feature I've seen was a well-documented escape hatch, not a well-designed golden path."*

## Self-assess (30 min)

### From the repo

```bash
prep quiz --topic interview-behavioral
```

Target: comfort with the question set, not a specific score. The quiz is more "have you thought about these" than "can you recite answers."

### Reflection (final — write your answers)

1. Pick one of your 8 stories that still feels a little off. What specifically is unclear when you tell it — the setup, the decision point, or the outcome?
2. Of the 5 closing questions you prepared, which three will you use regardless of the interviewer? Which one is specifically for an engineering manager, and which is for an IC peer?
3. Write one sentence that captures *why you, specifically, are the right person for this kind of role*. If it sounds generic, rewrite it until it couldn't apply to anyone else.

## The last 48 hours

1. **Do not add new material.** No new reading. No new practice. At this point, rehearsing more creates over-polish, not better performance.
2. **Review your stories once, then stop.** Re-read your index cards / notes one time the day before. That's it.
3. **Sleep.** Interviews are cognitive; sleep is the single biggest variable you can control.
4. **Do something unrelated.** Walk, cook, whatever clears your head. Platform engineering interviews are long; you want to arrive fresh, not saturated.
5. **If you have a commute or travel:** have your stories in a lightweight format (a single page of notes) to review briefly, not a deep-work document to obsess over.

---

## After the loop

1. **Debrief once.** Write down: questions you were asked, how you did, what you'd do differently, any signal from the interviewers.
2. **Stop ruminating.** If you catch yourself looping on a specific answer, write the better version down, then move on.
3. **Regardless of outcome — you prepared.** This pack is a season of work. The work doesn't become invisible if you didn't get this role. The next interview is closer because of it.
